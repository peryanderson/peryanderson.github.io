Responsive Design and Fluid Layouts

Brief thoughts on responsive design using responsive design

By: Peter Jang and Perry Anderson

Responsive Design

Responsive design is simply, design to the user. It involves several different facets, such as reformatting the information on a siter based on what kind of device it is being used. The philosophy of responsive design is that a site should respond to how the user interacts with it. A List Apart and The Treehouse Blog both do an excellent job going over the basics of responsive design. Max Design has a good example of simple HTML and CSS to show how the mechanics of media queries work, which is at the heart of responsive design.

Fluid Layouts

Fluid layouts are layouts that are based on fluid girds, that is the size of particular sections are defined as percentages of the screen that they occupy, rather than pixel lengths. This allows for resizing and responsive design. Fluid layouts do not on their own constitue representative design, but they are an important element of it. Unstoppable Robot Ninja has a good article which goes over the mechanics of how to achieve fluid layouts.

Conclusion

Responsive design has become increasingly important due to the greatly increased types of screens that people use - from the smallest cell phone screens to the largest 4k monitors. Responsive design isn't always the best approach - directing users to alternate mobile sites is also a viable option. Responsive design and fluid layouts give a lot of flexibility, but make it difficult from a graphic design perspective. Pixel accurate image design becomes difficult to achieve when having to plan for so many different layouts.

  • How do you think smartphones and tablets have changed web design? How do you think new technology will change web design in the future?

    • They have obviously had a huge impact. A greater variety of devices on which to view web sites means a greater demand on designers to make their work compatible with a huge new variety of technology. As for the future, it is always hard to predict. If the current trends continue we can expect people to consume media in even more varied ways. Content will continue to be designed for viewing on specific devices, but responsive design will become more and more important

  • How do you think visually impaired people use the web? Can they? Should we design and development for them?

    • I can't speak intelligently about what it is like to experience the internet, or the world, as a visually impaired person. Helping the visually impaired is certainly noble, and this includes the development of tools, whether they be programs or physical devices, that allow them to better interact with the internet.
